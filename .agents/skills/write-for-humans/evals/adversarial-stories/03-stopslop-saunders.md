# The Onboarding

*Author channeled: George Saunders (tender-absurd satirist of late-capitalist American sweetness)*
*Source rules violated: hardikpandya/stop-slop*

---

At its core, orientation day at SplendidCorp is about belonging — and that's okay. What makes this strange is how the belonging feels less like welcome and more like a soft, carpeted shove. Every employee receives a lanyard. Every lanyard receives a name. Every name receives, fundamentally, a destiny.

The decision to hire me emerged from the data. The data tells us I am a good fit. The market rewards good fits — full stop. Let that sink in.

I sit at Desk Nine. Nine desks. Nine chairs. Nine motivational posters about Resilience. The air smells like printer toner and, I promise, something creeps in underneath it, something like grief. But we'll unpack that later. In this section, we'll navigate onboarding together. I want to walk you through it.

Todd from HR approaches. Todd is a man who has, over many years, stopped being a person and started being a deliverable. His smile is a game-changer. What he says is: *Welcome, champ.* What he means is something else entirely, and the real question is whether I am brave enough to ask.

Here's the thing: I am not.

Instead, I double down on nodding. The conversation moves toward benefits. Benefits are significant. The implications are significant. The stakes are high. Nobody designed this, and yet here we all are, pretending — with our little plastic badges, with our little ergonomic footrests — that the pretending is the job.

A memo arrives. The memo announces itself. The memo tells us, in its bureaucratic way, that morale has shifted upward. Morale, apparently, has legs. Morale walked into the building this morning and decided things. Interestingly, nobody saw it come in.

Lunch is provided. Sandwiches. Chips. Regret. I eat at the communal table, where a woman named Brenda is crying without making any sound. What do the tears want? Release. What does Brenda want? A different Tuesday. What does SplendidCorp want? Brenda, but quieter.

At the end of the day, when it comes to corporate life in a world where every interaction has been optimized, the truth is this: we are all, always, everywhere, performing a version of ourselves that was assembled by committee. This is genuinely hard. This is what being a person actually looks like now. Period.

Todd returns. Todd has, over the course of the afternoon, located my soul on a spreadsheet. He leans in. *Moving forward,* he says, *we're going to lean into your authenticity.* I nod. The nodding is the deliverable. The deliverable is the nod.

Walking to the parking garage, I pass Brenda again. She is on the phone, laughing now, not a real laugh but the laugh you use when your manager is within earshot. It's not a laugh. It's a résumé. The laugh isn't the problem. The listening is.

Nobody gets to be themselves here. Everyone knows this. No one says it. The conversation always circles back to synergy. The bet lives or dies in the Q3 review. The culture shifts, or doesn't, depending on the slide deck.

And here's what I find interesting: I love it. I genuinely do. I simply love being told who I am. I really do. It's easier than the alternative, and that's okay.

The elevator descends. Twelve floors. Twelve small deaths. Twelve rehearsals for the next thing.

Let me be clear: this is what resilience actually looks like.
