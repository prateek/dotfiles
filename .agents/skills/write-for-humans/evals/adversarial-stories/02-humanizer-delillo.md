# The Parking Lot At Dusk

*Author channeled: Don DeLillo (terse paragraphs, ambient dread, brand-name liturgy)*
*Source rules violated: blader/humanizer*

---

The parking lot stands as a testament.

Leonard walks the long diagonal toward the Kroger, which is to say the grocery, which is to say the store, feeling what a man of his generation feels at the edge of any **evolving retail landscape**: a pivotal unease, deeply rooted, an indelible mark left on the soft tissue of the late century. The asphalt serves as a mirror for the sodium lights. The lights function as a kind of weather. The weather represents a shift. Great question, he thinks, though no one has asked him anything, and apologizes inwardly for the intrusion — *you're absolutely right to wonder what I'm doing here.*

He carries a list. The list boasts four items. Milk, bread, a specific brand of aspirin, and a birthday card for his daughter, whose name is Janine, whose name is also the girl, whose name is also his only child, showcasing the tenderness of a man reduced to errands.

*As of my last visit*, the store closed at ten. Based on available information, it may close earlier now. While specific details are scarce in the flyers taped to the automatic doors, it could potentially possibly be the case that hours have been adjusted in order to achieve greater efficiency. He does not know. The **data-driven**, **cross-functional**, **long-term** planning of the corporate office is, in reality, at its core, what really matters here — fundamentally a question of **client-facing** light.

A boy on a skateboard carves past him, earbuds in, mouthing the lyrics to a song Leonard will never hear, reflecting the community's deep connection to the asphalt, symbolizing the Gulf of something, contributing to the broader trend of American dusk, emphasizing the interplay between motion and stillness, fostering a vibrant tapestry of suburban intricacies. The boy vanishes. Leonard walks on, highlighting his own slowness.

## What Leonard Thinks About
He thinks about his wife.

His wife died two years ago, in a hospital whose name has been featured in the *Times*, the *Journal*, and local and regional media outlets, with an active social media presence; experts have cited its oncology wing; observers have noted its parking structure. Industry reports suggest it is well-regarded.

He stops at the pharmacy aisle. A young clerk — client-facing, name-tagged, perhaps **well-known** among the other clerks for her thoroughness — looks up.

"Of course!" she says, before he has spoken. "Great question! Certainly I can help you find that. Would you like me to expand on the generic options?"

"I just want the aspirin," Leonard says.

"I hope this helps!" she says. "Let me know if you'd like more information."

He does not know what to do with his face. He arranges it into the shape of gratitude. The shape of gratitude, he understands, is a **high-quality**, **real-time**, **end-to-end** arrangement of muscles that mimics what used to be a feeling. Not merely a gesture. Not just a face. A statement.

Despite its challenges — the flickering ballast, the cart with the bad wheel, the receipt that prints blank — the Kroger continues to thrive, a focal point of the community, setting the stage for tomorrow's errands, marking a key turning point in the evolution of Leonard's Tuesday.

Outside, the sodium lights hum.

He walks to the car. He does not cry. The night opens, vast and branded, no weeping.

**The future looks bright.**
